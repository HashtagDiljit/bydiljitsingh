Thin Plate with Hole — FEA Mesh Study (Abaqus)

FEA of a thin Inconel 718 plate with a central hole under 20 kN tension, then 85 kN after convergence. Mesh sweep from 46 to 8,890 elements; validation against stress-concentration theory.

Solo project Role: FEA Analyst Duration: [insert dates] Tools: Abaqus/CAE

Context & Challenge

A plate-with-hole is a classic FEA benchmark. Here, an Inconel 718 plate is tensioned to 20 kN (and then 85 kN) to study how mesh density affects the predicted peak stress at the hole. The model uses quarter symmetry to cut solve time without losing fidelity.

Objectives & KPIs

Accuracy

Converge σmax ≈ 1.7×10⁸ Pa near ~1000 elements.

Efficiency

Keep solve time around ~8 s at the optimal mesh (600–1200 elems).

Validation

Check against Kt ≈ 2.06 theory → σmax,th ≈ 1.79×10⁸ Pa.

Robustness

Symmetry BCs and displacement contours reviewed.

My Role & Method

I set up the model, applied symmetry boundary conditions, swept five mesh densities (46 → 8890 elements), logged runtime and σmax, validated vs. theory, and then re-ran at 85 kN using the optimal mesh.

“Find the point where more elements no longer change the answer — then ship the right mesh, not the biggest mesh.”

Technical Contributions

Geometry & Boundary Conditions

Quarter model with symmetry on the mid-planes; pressure load applied to the tension face. This reduces DOFs while preserving the hotspot around the hole.

Quarter model & BCs for efficiency.

Material Model

Inconel 718: E = 200 GPa, ν = 0.3; yield ≈ 1186 MPa, UTS ≈ 1400 MPa (context for stress levels).

Meshing Strategy

Auto-meshing used consistently across five ranges: 46, 230, 982, 4028, 8890 elements. Convergence observed near ~1000 elements with diminishing returns beyond that.

Results & Validation

σmax rose from 1.36×10⁸ Pa (46 elems) to 1.73×10⁸ Pa (8890 elems). Using Kt ≈ 2.06, the closed-form estimate is ≈1.79×10⁸ Pa — close to the converged FEA result.

Convergence: accuracy ↑ then plateaus; runtime rises after ~1000 elems.

Reflections & Learnings

  • Mesh convergence beats brute-force meshing for speed and reliability.
  • Symmetry + simplified geometry can cut runtime dramatically.
  • Always benchmark against theory (Kt) for sanity checks.
