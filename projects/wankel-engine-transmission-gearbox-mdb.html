Wankel Engine Transmission — Aero-Engine Speed Reducer (MDB)

Designed a compact, serviceable gearbox to reduce Wankel engine speed from 6500–7000 rpm down to 2000 rpm at the propeller using a 3.25:1 gear ratio.

Team size: 3 Role: Gearbox Design (gears, shafts, bearings, seals) Duration: Oct–Dec 2024 Tools: Fusion 360, Ansys Fluent, Granta Edupack

Context & Challenge

For an aero-engine application, the Wankel engine operates at high speed but the propeller must run near 2000 rpm for efficiency and noise. The challenge was to engineer a compact, reliable speed reducer meeting power (≈50 hp), packaging (≤90 mm centre distance; limited shaft overhang), durability (≥2000 h) and serviceability constraints.

Objectives & KPIs

Speed Reduction

Ratio 3.25:1 to achieve 2000 rpm at the propeller.

Reliability

Bearings selected for >2000 h life (L10h), with axial/radial load checks.

Safety

Withstand surge/vibration; shafts designed for fatigue with adequate factors of safety.

Packaging

Centre distance ≤ 90 mm; output shaft projection ≤ 90 mm.

My Role & Leadership

As a core designer, I sized the gear pair (via GP100), selected bearings, designed shafts, specified seals and lubrication, and authored the assembly drawing and report extracts. I also ran DFA to reduce parts and simplify assembly.

“Balanced performance, packaging and reliability to turn a high-speed Wankel into a prop-friendly drivetrain.”

  • 3 teammates in a compact design cell (MDB)
  • Delivered 1 assembly drawing pack & 1 technical report
  • Improved assembly with DFA: fewer parts, standardised fasteners

Technical Contributions

Gear Pair (3.25:1)

Ratio chosen to reduce 6500/7000 rpm to ~2000 rpm at the propeller. Helical geometry selected for smoothness and longevity; materials balanced (stronger pinion vs wheel) with manufacturable face width.

GP100 outputs confirming geometry and life targets.

Bearings & Life

Tapered rollers on the input (combined loads) and deep-groove ball bearings on the output (primarily radial). Equivalent load and L10h checks exceed the 2000 h requirement.

Bearing selection and L10h life calculations.

Shafts, Fatigue & Critical Speed

Shear/bending envelopes defined from gear forces; diameters tuned for FoS in the 4–6 band. Deflection and first critical speed checks confirm stiffness margins.

Free-body, shear and bending moment diagrams for sizing.

Seals, Lubrication & DFA

Spring-loaded rotary shaft seals with nitrile rubber; splash lubrication with trough and baffle (PLV >12.5 m/s). DFA steps reduced part count, unified fasteners and simplified assembly direction.

Exploded view showing serviceable layout.

Reflections & Learnings

  • Managing axial loads early drives bearing choice and shaft geometry.
  • DFA and fastener standardisation have outsized impact on build clarity.
  • Helical gears improve smoothness but add axial load—trade-offs matter.
  • Critical speed and deflection checks are essential for aero-use reliability.
