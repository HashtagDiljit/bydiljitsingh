Inhaler Spacer Redesign — CFD Study (Healthcare)

CFD-led redesign of a paediatric inhaler spacer to reduce oropharyngeal deposition, improve lung-bound fraction, and keep pressure drop low at typical breathing flow rates.

Team size: 4 Role: CFD Lead & Design Duration: [insert dates] Tools: Fusion 360, Ansys Fluent, Granta Edupack

Context & Challenge

Pressurised metered-dose inhalers (pMDIs) for children often suffer from poor drug delivery due to oropharyngeal deposition, coordination issues and high pressure drop. The brief was to pick an everyday medical product and redesign it using CFD to improve performance without sacrificing usability or cleanability.

Objectives & KPIs

Deposition

Reduce oropharyngeal deposition; increase lung-bound fine particle fraction.

Pressure Drop

Keep Δp within comfortable inhalation limits at 15–30 L/min.

Uniformity

Smooth, low-swirl velocity profile at mouthpiece to aid delivery.

Usability

Low part count, easy to clean, no added valves.

My Role & Method

I led the CFD work: baseline assessment, CAD of spacer concepts (cone + diffuser + baffle variations), meshing and inflation-layer strategy, turbulence model selection, particle tracking for droplet behaviour, and iteration to a design that balances Δp and deposition.

“Shape the flow before the mouthpiece — tame swirl, avoid recirculation, and the drug reaches where it matters.”

  • 3 design loops (baseline → concept A/B → final)
  • Mesh-independence to < 1% change in Δp
  • Automated plots for Δp, velocity uniformity and deposition estimates

Technical Contributions

Geometry & Boundary Conditions

Spacer body with gentle diffuser, integrated flow straighteners near the mouthpiece, and optional anti-swirl baffle upstream. Inlet mass-flow or velocity profile for 15–30 L/min; pressure outlet; no-slip walls.

Cutaway of redesigned geometry (diffuser + straighteners).

Meshing Strategy

Poly-hexcore with inflation layers along walls and at baffle edges to capture boundary layers; refined core near the mouthpiece and in recirculation regions. Three meshes (coarse/med/fine) for independence.

Inflation near walls; graded refinement to the mouthpiece.

Turbulence & Particles

SST k–ω (steady RANS) with second-order schemes. Discrete Phase Model for droplets (polydisperse Rosin–Rammler), stochastic tracking and wall interaction tuned to estimate deposition trends.

Streamlines show reduced swirl in the final design.

Validation & Sensitivity

Compared Δp with correlations/benchmarks; sensitivity to flow rate and particle size distribution. Final design meets target Δp and improves predicted delivery uniformity at the mouthpiece.

Baseline vs. concepts A/B vs. final — Δp & uniformity.

Reflections & Learnings

  • Flow straighteners near the outlet improved velocity uniformity more than upstream baffles.
  • Local mesh near the mouthpiece matters most; uniform refinement is wasteful.
  • Particle modelling assumptions change deposition trends — validate ranges, not single numbers.
  • Next: test anti-static materials and transient puff synchronisation with breathing profiles.
