Small-Scale Two-Stroke Engine — Manufacturing & Design (MDA)

Single-cylinder two-stroke CI engine for a UAV: 12,000 rpm, 45 W. I led the manufacturing plan, material selection, CAM toolpaths and assembly jigs for a 20,000-unit build, and validated critical speed & safety factors.

Team size: 3 Role: Manufacturing & CAM Lead Duration: Oct–Dec 2023 Tools: Fusion 360, Fusion 360 CAM, Granta Edupack

Context & Challenge

Design and plan manufacture for a compact two-stroke engine to power a UAV for at least 10 minutes. Constraints included 12,000 rpm operation, tight mass/size targets, safe operation and a scalable build plan for 20,000 units in 4 months.

Objectives & KPIs

Performance

Run at 12,000 rpm, output ~45 W; bore/stroke 9/11 mm.

Reliability

Critical speed ≥ 1.6× running rpm; safety factor 2.3–3.5.

Manufacturability

Processes for high-volume: turning, milling, casting, drilling; jigs & DFA.

Materials

AISI 1050 for crank/rod; grey cast iron piston/case; PP fuel tank.

My Role & Contributions

I owned the manufacturing plan and CAM, led materials selection via Granta, and authored assembly jigs/flows. I also resolved geometry clashes (rod–piston/case), re-balanced bore:stroke and crankshaft dimensions to meet critical-speed and deflection targets, and packaged the deliverables (reports, drawings, CAM).

“I turned a student prototype into a buildable product — cutting data, jigs, and flows that scale to 20k units.”

  • 3 teammates coordinated across design, manufacture, validation
  • Delivered 2 reports + 1 CAM pack + assembly drawings
  • Critical speed lifted to ≥1.6× run rpm with FoS 2.3–3.5

Technical Contributions

Architecture & PDS

Single-cyl 2-stroke CI, air-cooled; target **12,000 rpm**, **45 W**; B:S **0.8:1**; CI fuel; finish & safety requirements; maintainability plan.

PDS highlights for performance, safety and materials.

Crankshaft Design & Validation

Adopted AISI 1050; adjusted rod radius and bearing spacing to increase stiffness and reduce bending. Used Rayleigh–Ritz for critical speed; Tresca for FoS; achieved FoS ~2.3–3.5 and critical speed ≥1.6× run rpm.

Deflection & critical-speed checks drive geometry edits.

Piston & Conrod Manufacture

Piston (grey cast iron): turning + drilling + deburr; cycle ~1:57. Conrod (AISI 1050): CNC milling + ream; cycle ~1:44. Introduced a piston jig for consistent gudgeon-pin press fit.

Process plans & jigs to stabilise quality and cycle time.

Casing, Head & Back Plate

Crankcase: gravity die cast + milling/drilling; head & back plate: turned & drilled. Head cycle ~2:24, back plate ~1:23. Added chamfers/fillets to cut stress risers and aid machining.

Casting for volume; machining for interfaces & finish.

CAM & Cutting Data

Example component (⌀9.02 × 8.5 mm): 3-axis milling strategy. Rough/finish with 3 mm flat endmill; drill 4 mm; total cycle ~2:15; Ra 0.4–1.6 μm as needed. Fixturing via sacrificial stock tab; flood coolant.

CAM program with ops, feeds/speeds and fixturing.

Assembly Flow & DFA

Sub-assemblies (piston, crankshaft, fuel system) with jigs; final assembly via fasteners + press ops only. DFA simplified fasteners and assembly direction; inspection & packaging steps standardised.

Sub-assembly jigs improve repeatability at scale.

Reflections & Learnings

  • Critical-speed and deflection checks must drive geometry early.
  • Simple jigs save time and scrap — worth the upfront effort.
  • DFA and fastener standardisation clean up assembly flow.
  • CAM realism: fixturing and Ra targets matter as much as feeds/speeds.
